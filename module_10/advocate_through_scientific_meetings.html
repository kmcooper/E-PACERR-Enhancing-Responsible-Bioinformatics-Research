Science is a Community Activity

Scientific discovery rarely occurs in isolation. While publications communicate completed research, much of the process of refining ideas, challenging assumptions, and generating new collaborations occurs through interactions with other researchers. Graduate students and early-career scientists often begin learning these skills through journal clubs, departmental seminars, laboratory meetings, and internal presentations before participating in regional, national, or international conferences.

Key idea: Conferences are not simply venues to present research—they are opportunities to become part of the scientific community. You can become a good advocate for science by taking part in this practice - visitng conferences, seminars, workshops, birds of a feather, journal clubs, and other means for discourse.

What Does Research Say About Conferences?

Research on academic conferences consistently demonstrates that they provide value far beyond the presentation of completed work.

Benefits identified by Hansen & Budtz Pedersen (2018)

In their literature review published in Research Evaluation, Hansen and Budtz Pedersen found that academic conferences support multiple important functions, including:

  • Dissemination of research
  • Knowledge exchange
  • Networking
  • Collaboration
  • Career development
  • Community building

Perhaps most importantly, they concluded that many of the greatest benefits occur during informal social interactions, including conversations between sessions, poster discussions, introductions to new colleagues, and networking during meals or social events.

Scientific and Social Benefits

Hauss (2021) surveyed doctoral students and postdoctoral researchers regarding their conference experiences. Participants consistently reported both scientific and social benefits.

Scientific Benefits

  • Receiving constructive feedback on research
  • Learning about new methods and technologies
  • Discovering emerging research directions
  • Improving oral presentation skills

Social Benefits

  • Meeting colleagues and future collaborators
  • Increasing professional visibility
  • Developing a sense of belonging within the scientific community
  • Learning disciplinary expectations, norms, and culture

These findings suggest that conferences play an important role in helping early-career researchers develop not only technical expertise but also their professional identity.

Conferences as Places Where Knowledge Develops

Gross and Fleming (2011) argue that conferences should not simply be viewed as places where finished research is presented. Instead, conferences are environments where scientific knowledge itself is refined through discussion.

Researchers present ideas that are then:

  • questioned,
  • challenged,
  • refined,
  • placed into broader context, and
  • improved through repeated interactions with other experts.
Many of the most valuable scientific discussions occur outside the formal presentation itself—in hallway conversations, after-question discussions, poster sessions, and repeated interactions across multiple conferences.

These interactions often influence future experiments, publications, collaborations, and even how researchers think about their work.

Conferences Are Not Perfect

Although conferences provide many benefits, they also present challenges.

Rowe (2018) identified several barriers that may prevent participants from fully benefiting from conference attendance:

  • Financial cost
  • Travel burden
  • Time away from research or teaching
  • Networking opportunities that may not align with one's research interests
  • Information overload due to large amounts of material presented in a short period of time

Importantly, the paper concludes that conferences are not automatically successful simply because researchers attend them. Instead, the value of the experience depends on how participants engage with both the scientific program and the broader community.

Making the Most of Conference Attendance

While conference quality and participant engagement vary, there are several strategies that can help maximize the benefits of attending any scientific meeting.

Before the Conference

  • Review the conference schedule and identify sessions of interest.
  • Read abstracts from researchers you hope to meet.
  • Prepare a brief description ("elevator pitch") of your own research.

During the Conference

  • Ask thoughtful questions.
  • Visit poster sessions.
  • Introduce yourself to researchers whose work interests you.
  • Participate in networking and social events.
  • Take notes on ideas, methods, and potential collaborations.

After the Conference

  • Follow up with new contacts.
  • Review your notes while they are still fresh.
  • Consider how new ideas might influence your own research.

Key Takeaways

  • Academic conferences support much more than research dissemination.
  • They facilitate networking, collaboration, mentoring, and professional identity development.
  • Informal conversations are often among the most valuable aspects of conference participation.
  • Conferences help shape scientific knowledge by providing opportunities for discussion and refinement of ideas.
  • Thoughtful planning and active participation can substantially increase the value of conference attendance.

Reflection

Reflection Questions
  1. What aspects of a conference do you think would be most valuable at your current career stage?
  2. How could informal conversations influence the direction of a research project?
  3. If you were attending your first conference, what strategies would you use to maximize your learning and networking opportunities?
  4. How might conference participation contribute to the broader scientific community beyond presenting your own work?
